Deep neural networks have demonstrated exceptional capability across various domains, yet, how they reason and represent complex environments remains unclear. Although recent work has shown that models can implicitly track game states in such domains, less work has been done to compare the emergent spatial representations of different baseline architectures. This raises a question: How differently do these networks reason and form internal representations when presented with minimal information? We investigate this by benchmarking Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs), Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and Transformers on the task of static chess position evaluation without human knowledge and game rules. To decouple spatial geometric variance from the combinatorial complexity of the board, we introduce Mean Spatial Reach (MSR), which we show to be a stronger diagnostic metric than simply using the number of legal moves. Additionally, we use linear probing to show the differences in their latent representations and use saliency maps to help explain network predictions.
\end{abstract}

% --- Section 1: Introduction ---
\section{Introduction}

Deep learning has been applied to numerous disciplines due to its advanced capabilities in tackling complex tasks. However, the inner mechanics driving decisions that deep neural networks make are difficult to interpret, an issue that has become an active field of research in recent years. Attempts in explainable artificial intelligence (XAI) in fields like natural language processing and computer vision have become increasingly successful \cite{alain2016understanding, belinkov2022probing}. Unfortunately, those networks are often trained on noisy, continuous or unstructured data. Studies also regularly mix domain-specific knowledge into baseline architectures. As a result, the architecture's inductive biases become entangled with human-engineered priors, making it harder to determine what the models actually learned from the raw data when making predictions.

To get a better understanding of some of the most commonly used baseline architectures, we need a domain that is structured, complex, yet provides perfect information to the network. A board game like chess fits these criteria perfectly. Moreover, it provides an environment with a set of strict rules that allows us to conduct various probing tests \cite{toshniwal2022chess}. Hence, we believe this is a great opportunity to observe how different architectures generalize and discover spatial geometric patterns, which we refers to as the physical 2D arrangement of pieces, open space and long-range interactions, all without any knowledge of game rules.

This leads to our central question: Are deep networks able to internalize the underlying rules and piece relationships of chess without context or search? If so, how differently do they form an internal representation with the limited information provided to them? 

In this paper, we evaluate three architectures using only the current board state and a ground-truth evaluation from the current state-of-the-art Stockfish engine. Beyond a simple Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) which we use as our non-spatial baseline, we focus on comparing a Squeeze-and-Excitation Convolutional Neural Network (SE-ResNet) \cite{he2016deep, hu2018squeeze} against an Encoder-only Transformer \cite{vaswani2017attention} to observe how their unique mechanics result in different emergent representations. 

Because the networks were trained without any knowledge of the game rules, we use linear probes to investigate their internal representations of piece mobility. We also introduce Mean Spatial Reach (MSR) as a metric that decouples the geometric layout of the board from its combinatorial complexity. By using MSR to analyze performance across a rigorously controlled subset of positions, we show that the Transformer's attention mechanism captures long-range spatial dependencies much better than convolutions even with the existence of the SE layer. Lastly, through the use of saliency maps, we visualize how these different architectures prioritize features when making predictions.

% --- Section 2: Related Work  ---
\section{Related Work}
\subsection{Neural Chess Engines}
Initial breakthroughs in computer chess \cite{lai2015giraffe, david2016deepchess} had shown that deep neural networks could replace manually tuned evaluation heuristics popular at that time. Those early models utilized MLPs, which remains as the backbone of Efficiently Updatable Neural Networks (NNUE) \cite{nasu2018efficiently} that powers state-of-the-art engines like Stockfish today. These engines leverage and improve on engineered input representations from previous generations such as piece to king relationships, along with advanced search algorithms to achieve superhuman play. Without these "add-ons", MLPs would see a flat input vector and have to learn chess concepts from scratch due to a lack of spatial information.

Later, CNNs emerged as a dominant architecture, as they are capable of capturing spatial relationships without the need for manual feature engineering \cite{silver2017alphazero}. Eventually, it was shown that transformer based models can completely and efficiently internalize chess concepts, achieving grandmaster-level of play without any search algorithm or injection of domain specific knowledge \cite{ruoss2024grandmaster}.

\subsection{Representation Learning}
Probing techniques \cite{alain2016understanding} and attribution methods like saliency maps \cite{simonyan2013deep} are often used to analyze and interpret models. These methods have been applied to perfect information games, showing that networks do learn human concepts \cite{mcgrath2022acquisition} and develop internal representations of board state \cite{li2022emergent}. 

Furthermore, it was shown that vision transformers and convolutional neural networks form uniquely different features and structures \cite{raghu2021vision}. Building on their work, we provide a comparative analysis that clearly displays the strength of the Transformer in discovering and encoding spatial representations under strict constraints.

% --- Section 3: Methodology  ---
\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Data} 
Our dataset consists of approximately 5.8M unique board states downloaded and extracted from CCRL website (\url{www.computerchess.org.uk/ccrl/}). Each state $s$ is run through Stockfish 17 at a depth of 20, which gives a centipawn score describing the position's evaluation $E(s)$. We frame position evaluation as a 7-class classification problem by binning the centipawn scores $E(s)$ into four magnitude tiers: Equal ($|E(s)| < 100$), Better ($100 \le |E(s)| < 300$), Decisive Advantage ($300 \le |E(s)| < 500$) and Winning ($|E(s)| \ge 500$). Furthermore, we apply Gaussian label smoothing on $E(s)$ to generate soft targets over the 7 classes (see \hyperref[label_smoothing]{Appendix A.0.1}).

To ensure these models generalize well, we apply three more steps during the curation of the dataset. Firstly, we follow the methodology of \textit{Giraffe} \cite{lai2015giraffe} by applying one random legal move to each position in our dataset which introduces imbalanced and unstructured positions. Next, to prevent the trivial result of networks simply guessing the "Equal" class, we construct a perfectly uniform class distribution for both training and testing by under-sampling the "Equal" class and selectively augmenting minority classes via horizontal board mirroring. Then, we filter out volatile positions ($<1\%$ of the dataset) where evaluations fluctuates by more than one class between Stockfish depths of 10 and 20.

Unlike standard neural chess engines that normalize inputs to the perspective of White, we preserve the absolute, unrotated board state so as to not inject the concept of spatial symmetry into training. While we also utilize horizontal board mirroring to augment our dataset, that merely samples from other valid, reachable game states. Finally, the dataset is split by games to prevent data leakage, with a ratio of 80/10/10 for training, validation and testing respectively. 

\subsection{Input Representation} 
We adopt a widely used multi-channel tensor for CNNs \cite{silver2017alphazero} and modify it accordingly for MLPs and Transformers. Because our models function as static evaluation functions for an alternating Markov game \cite{tang2024maia}, we do not include past game states and provide only the current position.

For CNNs, the board state is represented as a spatial feature stack: $\mathbf{X}_{\text{stack}} \in \{0, 1\}^{19 \times 8 \times 8}$. The first 12 planes represent the spatial presence of each piece type. The remaining 7 planes consist of 6 global state planes (filled with $1$s or $0$s indicating the side to move, castling rights, and check status) and an $8 \times 8$ one-hot encoding of the en-passant target square, if any.

For MLPs, we flatten the board into a 775-dimensional binary vector $\mathbf{x}_{\text{flat}} \in \{0,1\}^{775}$ by concatenating the 12 piece bitboards ($12 \times 64 = 768$) with a 7-dimensional vector $\mathbf{g} \in \{0, 1\}^{7}$ encoding the same information present in the spatial feature stack.

For Transformers, we avoided the typical way of representing the board as a tokenized FEN string because we want to establish a one-to-one mapping between sequence tokens and each of the 64 squares on the board. This way, it preserves the spatial geometry of the board. Hence, we simply reshape the $19 \times 8 \times 8$ feature stack into a sequence of 64 tokens, where each token $\mathbf{t}_i \in \{0, 1\}^{19}$ represents the complete state of a single square.

\subsection{Model Architectures}
For clarity and consistency throughout this paper, we adopt this naming convention: [Architecture]-[Depth], where the numerical suffix indicates the total number of hidden layers or computational blocks (i.e., MLP-5, SE-ResNet-16, and Transformer-6).

\textbf{Multi-Layer Perceptrons.}\enskip To establish a baseline, we implement a standard sequential MLP. We deliberately keep the architecture straightforward to observe how a network learns using just raw parameter capacity. As expected, the model overfitted very quickly in initial experiments. Hence, we apply Batch Normalization \cite{ioffe2015batch} and Dropout \cite{srivastava2014dropout} after each layer. We experimented with different depths and widths, but found that these modifications yielded diminishing returns (see \hyperref[mlp_details]{Appendix A.1.1} for exact formulations and ablations).

\textbf{Convolutional Neural Networks.}\enskip We implement a deep residual neural network (ResNet) \cite{he2016deep} with Squeeze-and-Excitation (SE) blocks \cite{hu2018squeeze}, similar to older versions of the then state-of-the-art Leela Chess Zero engine. The raw input state $\mathbf{X}_{\text{stack}} \in \{0, 1\}^{19 \times 8 \times 8}$ is first projected to a higher-dimensional feature space via an initial convolution. It is then passed through a series of residual blocks, each containing two $3 \times 3$ convolutional layers followed by an SE block. Additionally, we apply Stochastic Depth \cite{huang2016deep} ($\text{DropPath}$) to the residual branches during training to regularize the network. We use global average pooling in both our SE blocks and the final classifier head, as this approach outperformed standard feature flattening in our initial experiments (see \hyperref[cnn_details]{Appendix A.1.2} for exact formulations and ablations).

\textbf{Transformers.}\enskip We implement an Encoder-only Transformer without providing it any information on how each square relates to another. First, the tokens $\mathbf{t}_i \in \{0, 1\}^{19}$ are linearly projected into a higher-dimensional embedding space, followed by a ReLU activation and Layer Normalization. However, as chess is not permutation invariant but self-attention is, we initialize the network with randomized 1D positional embeddings to allow the independent discovery of square relationships. The sequence is then passed through a tower of Transformer encoder blocks. Here, we utilize DeepNorm \cite{wang2024deepnet} over PreNorm as we found it yielded better results while maintaining similar training stability. Finally, the sequence of 64 updated square tokens is aggregated via global average pooling to produce a new state representation, which is then passed to a standard linear classification head. (see Appendix \hyperref[transformer_details]{Appendix A.1.3} for exact formulations and ablations).

\subsection{Evaluation and Analysis}
We constrain the parameter capacity of all evaluated models to approximately 1.2M to ensure fair comparisons. We use standard top-1 accuracy and off-by-1 accuracy (predicting an adjacent class) to evaluate each model. Additionally, we apply the following methods for analysis:


\textbf{Mean Spatial Reach.}\enskip Standard metrics like piece count and legal moves (branching factor) provide a great way to quantify the combinatorial complexity of a position. However, they fail to capture its geometric layout. Furthermore, quantification of boards using the number of legal moves is highly volatile depending on the side to move. The architectural inductive biases can be better understood when we decouple this combinatorial complexity from spatial complexities. Hence, we introduce Mean Spatial Reach (MSR), which quantifies the average spatial control (line of sight) pieces have over the board. For $N$ pieces, we compute the Chebyshev distance to every valid square $s$ a piece $p$ attacks or defends, including empty squares to account for the control of open space:
\[MSR = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{p=1}^{N} \sum_{s \in attacks(p)} \text{Chebyshev}(p, s)\]

As the game progresses and pieces are captured, the board density and branching factor of positions generally drops. For instance, in a dense middlegame, tight pawn structures often result in the lack of long-range interactions, causing the MSR of that position to be low. On the other hand, in open-board late games, the presence of long-range pieces likely increases the MSR of that position.

To be able to conduct tests where the spatial variance (the variance of the spatial control different positions have in the dataset) varies widely while not have it overshadowed by combinatorial complexity, we construct a heavily stratified open-board environment (Material $\in [0, 32]$, Piece Count $\in [2, 16]$) from our test dataset. We further stratify this dataset by fixing three control variables: Total Material Score, Total Piece Count, and Sliding Piece Ratio, which we shall refer to as the Spatial Variance Benchmark. Additionally, we remove positions containing Knights from the benchmark. This is because Knights have a fixed spatial reach of $d=2$, which suppresses the metric's dynamic range. Moreover, it introduces combinatorial noise, such as generating up to 8 legal moves without contributing to scalable spatial geometry. These stratifications ensures any variance in model performance would be due to changes in spatial arrangements, allowing us to better evaluate the differences in spatial inductive biases of our models (see \hyperref[sec:msr_stratification]{Appendix C} for full stratification methodology). 

\textbf{Linear Probing.}\enskip Because the networks were trained as static evaluation functions without knowledge of the game rules, we probe their latent representations to determine if any linearly separable concepts regarding piece movements emerged. Initial experiments showed that a single linear probe lacked the non-linearity required to internalize how every single piece moved simultaneously. Hence, we train piece-specific linear probes to predict the legality of moves to all possible squares given a position. For each probe, we train it with two different sets of data. Firstly, we balance the dataset such that 50\% of the samples evaluate the target piece, while the other 50\% evaluates non-target pieces (e.g. evaluates a Bishop for a Knight probe) or empty squares, in which moves to any square are labeled as illegal. This tests if the networks can simultaneously identify piece presence and determine legal moves. Secondly, we modify the dataset such that 100\% of the samples evaluate the target piece. This is done so that the probe focuses entirely on separating possible representations of piece movements. The probes are attached to the deepest possible parts before dimensionality reduction significantly degrades representations (see \hyperref[probe_locations]{Appendix B.1}). Lastly, we also train a control probe with raw bitboards by passing the flattened state vector (the same input provided to the MLP) directly into the linear classification probe.

\textbf{Saliency Maps.}\enskip We can use gradients calculated from backpropagation to give possible explanations of network predictions by visualizing input sensitivity with saliency maps~\cite{simonyan2013deep}. Unlike standard RGB images, where we commonly take the maximum gradient across the color channels, the inputs we described previously are sparse and mutually exclusive (a square can only contain one piece). To ensure we do not discard meaningful information about any potential piece when determining the total input sensitivity of each square, we construct the saliency map $M \in \mathbb{R}^{8 \times 8}$ by summing the magnitude of the gradients across all 12 piece channels:
\[M_{ij} = \sum_{k=1}^{12} | \mathbf{W}_{kij} |\]
where $\mathbf{W}_{kij}$ denotes the gradient element for piece channel $k$ at rank $i$ and file $j$.

Instead, the advantage itself, is due to the ability to better resolve long-range interactions.

\autoref{fig:linear_probe_comparison_bishop} reveal three differences that highlight the architectural inductive biases which affect spatial representations:

\textbf{Target Square Resolution.}\enskip The rule of not being able to capture friendly pieces is a fundamental rule in Chess. However, the representations formed by the CNN (and the MLP) is not linearly decodable, resulting in the high probabilities assigned to squares occupied by pieces of the same color as seen in \autoref{fig:linear_probe_comparison_bishop_cnn}. In contrast, the Transformer's latent space preserves this concept linearly, allowing the probe to suppress activations easily on friendly pieces as shown in \autoref{fig:linear_probe_comparison_bishop_transformer}.

\textbf{Piece occlusion.}\enskip The representations must contain linearly decodable information regarding piece occlusion (pieces blocking the line of sight of the target piece) for the probe to accurately determine long-range piece mobility. This conditional logic is is not linearly accessible in the CNN's (and MLP's) latent representations, causing the probe to project "movement rays" directly through pieces seen in \autoref{fig:linear_probe_comparison_bishop_cnn}. Although the Transformer Probe depict arguably perfect results in \autoref{fig:linear_probe_comparison_bishop_transformer}, it often leaves residual probability on the immediate square behind the occluder before completely dropping to zero (see \hyperref[sec:appendix_linear_probes]{Appendix E}). 

\textbf{Spatial Fading.}\enskip In the CNN's latent space, the linearly accessible information regarding legal moves steadily decays as the Chebyshev distance from the target piece increases. We hypothesize that this is the result of feature  degradation caused by routing these long-range piece dynamics through multiple layers and overlapping receptive fields of the inherent $3 \times 3$ convolutional kernels. In contrast, the Transformer probe is able to output uniform and high probabilities for squares even after increasing distance. By establishing an $O(1)$ spatial routing path between any two squares within a single attention layer, the Transformer is able bypass the problem of feature degradation seen in the CNN.

\subsubsection{Gaussian Label Smoothing}
\label{label_smoothing}
The centipawn score $E(s)$ is mapped to $c(E) \in [0, 6]$ with this interpolation function:
\[
c(E) =
\begin{cases}
\max\left(0.0, 0.5 - \frac{E - 500}{200}\right) & \text{if } E \ge 500 \\
1.5 - \frac{E - 300}{200} & \text{if } 300 \le E < 500 \\
2.5 - \frac{E - 100}{200} & \text{if } 100 \le E < 300 \\
3.5 - \frac{E + 100}{200} & \text{if } -100 \le E < 100 \\
4.5 - \frac{E + 300}{200} & \text{if } -300 \le E < -100 \\
5.5 - \frac{E + 500}{200} & \text{if } -500 < E \le -300 \\
\min\left(6.0, 5.5 - \frac{E + 500}{200}\right) & \text{if } E \le -500
\end{cases}\]
Then, we use a normalized Gaussian distribution centered at $c(E)$ to generate the soft target probability distribution of the 7 classes:
\[p_i = \frac{\exp\left( - \frac{(i - c(E))^2}{2\sigma^2} \right)}{\sum_{j=0}^{6} \exp\left( - \frac{(j - c(E))^2}{2\sigma^2} \right)}\]
The networks are trained to minimize the cross-entropy loss between their predictions and the soft target distribution. Here, we choose $\sigma = 0.6$ over standard label smoothing ($\sigma = 0.1$) to ensure the true target class retains a majority of the probability mass ($\approx66\%$) and also allocates a meaningful weight ($\approx16.5\%$ each) to the adjacent classes. This gave better results as shown below.

\subsubsection{CNN}
\label{cnn_details}
Let the main convolutional branch of a residual block be denoted as $\mathcal{F}$, where $\mathcal{F}(\mathbf{x}) = \text{BN}(\mathbf{W}_2 * \text{ReLU}(\text{BN}(\mathbf{W}_1 * \mathbf{x})))$. The forward pass for the $l$-th residual block is formally defined as:
$$\mathbf{h}^{(l)} = \text{ReLU}\Big( \text{DropPath}\big(\text{SE}(\mathcal{F}(\mathbf{h}^{(l-1)}, \mathbf{W}^{(l)}))\big) + \mathbf{h}^{(l-1)} \Big)$$
where $\mathbf{h}^{(l-1)}$ is the intermediate feature map from the previous layer.

During training, DropPath survival probabilities linearly decrease from $1.0$ at the first block to $0.8$ at the final block. The model we for the main evaluation (SE-ResNet-16) consists of 16 residual blocks, a channel depth of $C=64$, and an SE reduction ratio of $r=8$. The output of the residual blocks is projected to a small MLP with hidden dimensions of 256 with dropout of $0.5$ which then finally outputs the 7 class logits.

\subsubsection{Transformer}
\label{transformer_details}
Let MHA denote Multi-Head Self-Attention and FFN denote the Feed-Forward Network. The forward pass for the $l$-th encoder block using DeepNorm is formally defined as:
$$ \begin{aligned}
\mathbf{h}' &= \text{LN}\big(\mathbf{h}^{(l-1)} + \alpha \cdot \text{Dropout}(\text{MHA}(\mathbf{h}^{(l-1)}))\big) \\
\mathbf{h}^{(l)} &= \text{LN}\big(\mathbf{h}' + \alpha \cdot \text{Dropout}(\text{FFN}(\mathbf{h}'))\big)
\end{aligned} $$
where $\text{LN}$ is Layer Normalization and $\alpha$ is the DeepNorm residual scaling factor derived from the total network depth using the formula $\alpha = (2N)^{-0.25}$.

The hyperparameters for our baseline Transformer-6 model are: $D_{emb} = 128$, Number of Attention Heads $= 4$, Feed-Forward Network Dimension $= 512$, and Number of Encoder Blocks $= 6$.

\subsection{Usage of MSR}
We measure the correlation between MSR and the performance advantage ($\Delta_{acc}$) between the Transformer (TF) and the CNN by first dividing the dataset into $B = 15$ equal-sized quantile bins. For each bin $b$, we calculate the average accuracy of both models:

\[Acc_{TF}^{(b)} = \frac{1}{|b|} \sum_{i \in b} \mathbbm{1}(\text{TF}_i = \text{True})\]

\[Acc_{CNN}^{(b)} = \frac{1}{|b|} \sum_{i \in b} \mathbbm{1}(\text{CNN}_i = \text{True})\]

Then, we take the percentage difference to get $\Delta_{acc}$ for each bin: 
\[\Delta_{Acc}^{(b)} = \left( Acc_{TF}^{(b)} - Acc_{CNN}^{(b)} \right) \times 100\]

We then calculate the correlation using the Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression formula:
\[r = \frac{\sum_{b=1}^{B} (X_b - \overline{X})(Y_b - \overline{Y})}{\sqrt{\sum_{b=1}^{B} (X_b - \overline{X})^2 \sum_{b=1}^{B} (Y_b - \overline{Y})^2}}\]
where $\overline{X}$ and $\overline{Y}$ are the means of the binned variables. 

\subsection{Data Stratification}
Our goal is to isolate spatial variance in positions. Here, we present the filtering pipeline on our open-board subset of positions to curate the ideal test environment where MSR shines. We start with the original uncontrolled subset of open-board positions. 
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, noitemsep]
    \item \textbf{Test 1:} The original uncontrolled subset of open-board positions. 
    \item \textbf{Test 2:} Restrict the Total Material Score on the board to $\in [0, 32]$.
    \item \textbf{Test 3:} Restrict the Total Piece Count to $\in [2, 16]$.
    \item \textbf{Test 4:} Restrict the Sliding Piece Ratio, which is the fraction of long-range pieces (Queen, Bishop, Rook) over total pieces to $\in [0.15, 0.50]$. This is our Spatial Variance Benchmark. With the last restriction, it ensures variance in MSR would be mainly due to placements of the pieces rather than a trivial bias in piece types (e.g., a board containing more Queens than another).
\end{enumerate}
