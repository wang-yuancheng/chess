Deep neural networks have demonstrated exceptional capability across various domains, yet how they reason and represent complex environments remains unclear. Although recent work has shown that models can implicitly track game states in such domains, less has been done to compare the emergent spatial representations of different baseline architectures. We investigate this by benchmarking Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs), Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), and Transformers on the task of static chess position evaluation without a priori knowledge. Using linear concept probing and saliency maps, we highlight differences in their latent representations and rationalize network predictions. We further show, through the Spatial Variance Benchmark, that the superior performance of the Transformer as compared to the CNN is due to the attention mechanism's ability to capture scalable, long-range interactions better than convolutions.

\end{abstract}

% --- Section 1: Introduction ---
\section{Introduction}
An active field of research in recent years is interpreting deep neural networks to uncover the inner mechanics driving their predictions. These networks are often trained on noisy, continuous, or unstructured data. Studies also regularly mix domain-specific knowledge into baseline architectures. As a result, the architecture's inductive biases become entangled with human-engineered priors, making it harder to determine what the models actually learned from the raw data when making predictions. To get a better understanding of these networks, we need a domain that is structured, complex, yet provides perfect information to the network. Chess fits these criteria perfectly, providing an environment with a set of strict rules and complex tactical interactions.

In this paper, we use chess as a testbed to observe how different architectures and their inductive biases lead to different discoveries and representations of spatial geometric patterns, which we shall refer to as the physical 2D arrangement of pieces, open space and long-range interactions, all without any knowledge of the game rules. While it has been shown that networks can implicitly track game states \cite{li2022emergent} and achieve grandmaster-level play without search through the scaling of parameters \cite{ruoss2024grandmaster}, less is known about how the inductive biases of different architectures compare when forced to discover these concepts under parameter constraints.

This leads to our central question: How do the architectural inductive biases of different networks affect their ability to internalize the underlying rules and piece relationships of chess without context or search?

We evaluate three architectures using only the current board state and a ground-truth evaluation from the current state-of-the-art Stockfish engine. Beyond a simple Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) which we use as our non-spatial baseline, we focus on comparing a Squeeze-and-Excitation Convolutional Neural Network (SE-ResNet) \cite{he2016deep, hu2018squeeze} against an Encoder-only Transformer \cite{vaswani2017attention} to observe how their unique mechanisms result in different emergent representations. We also introduce Mean Spatial Reach (MSR) as a metric that decouples the geometric layout of the board from its combinatorial complexity. Using MSR, we construct a subset of test data which we call the Spatial Variance Benchmark, where we show that the Transformer's attention mechanism captures long-range spatial dependencies much better than convolutions even with the existence of the SE layer. Additionally, we leverage linear probes \cite{alain2016understanding} to investigate the different emergent internal representations of piece mobility. Lastly, using saliency maps \cite{simonyan2013deep}, we visualize and suggest reasons for these contrasting biases through two case studies of misclassifications.

\section{Related Work}
\subsection{Neural Chess Engines}
Initial breakthroughs in computer chess \cite{lai2015giraffe, david2016deepchess} had shown that deep neural networks could replace manually tuned evaluation heuristics. These early models utilized MLPs, which rely heavily on engineered input representations along with advanced search algorithms to achieve superhuman play. Without that support, MLPs would lack the inductive biases needed to process spatial information. 

Subsequently, CNNs emerged as a dominant architecture with their native inductive bias capable of capturing spatial relationships without the need for manual feature engineering \cite{silver2017alphazero}. Even more recently, transformer-based models have advanced into computer chess. It was shown that the injection of domain-specific spatial inductive bias do improve the evaluation and playing strength of the models \cite{monroe2024mastering, monroe2026chessformer}.

\subsection{Representation Learning}
Probing techniques \cite{alain2016understanding} and attribution methods like saliency maps \cite{simonyan2013deep} are often used to interpret models. These methods have been applied to perfect information games, showing that networks do learn human concepts \cite{mcgrath2022acquisition} and are able to form and track internal representations of board states \cite{li2022emergent}. 

Furthermore, it was shown that vision transformers and convolutional neural networks form uniquely different features and structures \cite{raghu2021vision}. Building on their work, we extend this comparison from standard image datasets to the domain of chess.

% --- Section 3: Methodology  ---
\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Data} 
Our dataset consists of approximately 5.8M unique board states downloaded and extracted from the CCRL website (\url{www.computerchess.org.uk/ccrl/}). Each state $s$ is run through Stockfish 17 at a depth of 20, which gives a centipawn score describing the position's evaluation $E(s)$. We frame position evaluation as a 7-class classification problem by binning the centipawn scores $E(s)$ into four magnitude tiers: Equal ($|E(s)| < 100$), Better ($100 \le |E(s)| < 300$), Decisive Advantage ($300 \le |E(s)| < 500$) and Winning ($|E(s)| \ge 500$). Furthermore, we apply Gaussian label smoothing on $E(s)$ to generate soft targets over the 7 classes (see \hyperref[label_smoothing]{Appendix A.0.1}).

To ensure these models generalize well, we apply three more steps during the curation of the dataset. Firstly, we follow the methodology of \textit{Giraffe} \cite{lai2015giraffe} by applying one random legal move to each position in our dataset, which introduces imbalanced and unstructured positions. Next, to prevent the trivial result of networks simply guessing the "Equal" class, we construct a perfectly uniform class distribution for both training and testing by under-sampling the "Equal" class and selectively augmenting minority classes via horizontal board mirroring. Then, we filter out volatile positions ($<1\%$ of the dataset) where evaluations fluctuate by more than one class between Stockfish depths of 10 and 20.

Unlike standard neural chess engines that normalize inputs to the perspective of White, we preserve the absolute, unrotated board state so as to not inject the concept of spatial symmetry into training. While we also utilize horizontal board mirroring to augment our dataset, which merely samples from other valid, reachable game states. Finally, the dataset is split by games to prevent data leakage, with a ratio of 80/10/10 for training, validation and testing respectively. 

\subsection{Input Representation} 
We adopt a widely used multi-channel tensor for CNNs \cite{silver2017alphazero} and modify it accordingly for MLPs and Transformers. Because our models function as static evaluation functions for an alternating Markov game \cite{tang2024maia}, we do not include past game states and provide only the current position.

For CNNs, the board state is represented as a spatial feature stack: $\mathbf{X}_{\text{stack}} \in \{0, 1\}^{19 \times 8 \times 8}$. The first 12 planes represent the spatial presence of each piece type. The remaining 7 planes consist of 6 global state planes (filled with $1$s or $0$s indicating the side to move, castling rights, and check status) and an $8 \times 8$ one-hot encoding of the en-passant target square, if any.

For MLPs, we flatten the board into a 775-dimensional binary vector $\mathbf{x}_{\text{flat}} \in \{0,1\}^{775}$ by concatenating the 12 piece bitboards ($12 \times 64 = 768$) with a 7-dimensional vector $\mathbf{g} \in \{0, 1\}^{7}$ encoding the same information present in the spatial feature stack.

For Transformers, we avoided the typical way of representing the board as a tokenized FEN string because we wanted to establish a one-to-one mapping between sequence tokens and each of the 64 squares on the board. This way, we preserve the spatial representation of the board. Hence, we simply reshape the $19 \times 8 \times 8$ feature stack into a sequence of 64 tokens, where each token $\mathbf{t}_i \in \{0, 1\}^{19}$ represents the complete state of a single square.

\subsection{Model Architectures}
For clarity and consistency throughout this paper, we adopt this naming convention: [Architecture]-[Depth], where the numerical suffix indicates the total number of hidden layers or computational blocks (i.e., MLP-5, SE-ResNet-16, and Transformer-6).

\textbf{Multi-Layer Perceptrons.}\enskip To establish a baseline, we implement a standard sequential MLP. We deliberately keep the architecture straightforward to observe how a network learns using just raw parameter capacity. As expected, the models overfitted very quickly in initial experiments. Hence, we apply Batch Normalization \cite{ioffe2015batch} and add Dropout \cite{srivastava2014dropout} after each layer. We experimented with different depths and widths, but found that these modifications yielded diminishing returns (see \hyperref[mlp_details]{Appendix A.1.1} for exact formulations and ablations).

\textbf{Convolutional Neural Networks.}\enskip We implement a deep residual neural network (ResNet) \cite{he2016deep} with Squeeze-and-Excitation (SE) blocks \cite{hu2018squeeze}, similar to older versions of the then-state-of-the-art Leela Chess Zero engine. The raw input state $\mathbf{X}_{\text{stack}} \in \{0, 1\}^{19 \times 8 \times 8}$ is first projected to a higher-dimensional feature space via an initial convolution. It is then passed through a series of residual blocks, each containing two $3 \times 3$ convolutional layers followed by an SE block. Additionally, we apply Stochastic Depth \cite{huang2016deep} ($\text{DropPath}$) to the residual branches during training to regularize the network. We use global average pooling in both our SE blocks and the final classifier head, as this approach outperformed standard feature flattening in our initial experiments (see \hyperref[cnn_details]{Appendix A.1.2} for exact formulations and ablations).

\textbf{Transformers.}\enskip We implement an Encoder-only Transformer without providing it with any information on how each square relates to another. First, the tokens $\mathbf{t}_i \in \{0, 1\}^{19}$ are linearly projected into a higher-dimensional embedding space, followed by a ReLU activation and Layer Normalization. However, as chess is not permutation invariant but self-attention is, so we initialize the network with randomized 1D positional embeddings to allow the independent discovery of square relationships. The sequence is then passed through a tower of Transformer encoder blocks. Here, we utilize DeepNorm \cite{wang2024deepnet} over PreNorm as we found it yielded better results while maintaining similar training stability. Finally, the sequence of 64 updated square tokens is aggregated via global average pooling to produce a new state representation, which is then passed to a standard linear classification head. (see Appendix \hyperref[transformer_details]{Appendix A.1.3} for exact formulations and ablations).

\subsection{Evaluation and Analysis}
We constrain the parameter capacity of all evaluated models to approximately 1.2M to ensure fair comparisons. We use standard top-1 accuracy and off-by-1 accuracy (predicting an adjacent class) to evaluate each model. Additionally, we apply the following methods for analysis:

\textbf{Mean Spatial Reach.}\enskip Standard metrics like piece count and legal moves (branching factor) provide a great way to quantify the combinatorial complexity of a position. However, they fail to capture its geometric layout. Furthermore, quantification of boards using the number of legal moves is highly volatile depending on the side to move. The architectural inductive biases can be better understood when we decouple this combinatorial complexity from spatial complexities. Hence, we introduce Mean Spatial Reach (MSR), which quantifies the average spatial control (line of sight) pieces have over the board. For $N$ pieces, we compute the Chebyshev distance to every valid square $s$ that a piece $p$ attacks or defends, including empty squares to account for the control of open space:
\[MSR = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{p=1}^{N} \sum_{s \in attacks(p)} \text{Chebyshev}(p, s)\]

As the game progresses and pieces are captured, the board density and branching factor of positions generally drop. For instance, in a dense middlegame, tight pawn structures often result in a lack of long-range interactions, causing the MSR of that position to be low. On the other hand, in open-board late games, the presence of long-range pieces likely increases the MSR of that position.

To be able to conduct tests where the spatial variance (the variance of the spatial control different positions have in the dataset) varies widely while not having it overshadowed by combinatorial complexity, we construct a heavily stratified open-board environment (Material $\in [0, 32]$, Piece Count $\in [2, 16]$) from our test dataset. We further stratify this dataset by fixing three control variables: Total Material Score, Total Piece Count, and Sliding Piece Ratio, which we shall refer to as the Spatial Variance Benchmark. Additionally, we remove positions containing Knights from the benchmark. This is because Knights have a fixed spatial reach of $d=2$, which suppresses the metric's dynamic range. Moreover, it introduces combinatorial noise, such as generating up to 8 legal moves without contributing to scalable spatial geometry. These stratifications ensure any variance in model performance would be due to changes in spatial arrangements, allowing us to better evaluate the differences in spatial inductive biases of our models (see \hyperref[sec:msr_stratification]{Appendix C} for full stratification methodology). 

\textbf{Linear Probing.}\enskip Because the networks were trained as static evaluation functions without knowledge of the game rules, we probe their latent representations to determine if any linearly separable concepts regarding piece movements emerged. Initial experiments showed that a single linear probe lacked the non-linearity required to internalize how every single piece moves simultaneously. Hence, we train piece-specific linear probes to predict the legality of moves to all possible squares given a position. For each probe, we train it with two different sets of data. Firstly, we balance the dataset such that 50\% of the samples evaluate the target piece, while the other 50\% evaluates non-target pieces (e.g. evaluates a Bishop for a Knight probe) or empty squares, in which moves to any square are labeled as illegal. This tests if the networks can simultaneously identify piece presence and determine legal moves. Secondly, we modify the dataset such that 100\% of the samples evaluate the target piece. This is done so that the probe focuses entirely on separating possible representations of piece movements. The probes are attached to the deepest possible parts before dimensionality reduction significantly degrades representations (see \hyperref[probe_locations]{Appendix B.1}). Lastly, we also train a control probe with raw bitboards by passing the flattened state vector (the same input provided to the MLP) directly into the linear classification probe.

\textbf{Saliency Maps.}\enskip We can use gradients calculated from backpropagation to give possible explanations of network predictions by visualizing input sensitivity with saliency maps~\cite{simonyan2013deep}. For each square, we sum the magnitude of the gradients across all 12 piece channels (see \hyperref[sec:saliency_maps]{Appendix D} for the details). While our MSR-based benchmark and linear probes provide quantitative evidence of the networks' spatial generalization and separability of concepts in the latent representation, they do not show qualitative context of failures due to limitations from their intrinsic inductive biases. Because attribution methods are susceptible to gradient fluctuations, especially in domains like chess where a single perturbation in the input can significantly alter predictions, we use them only to contextualize our quantitative findings. We present two case studies that stem from misclassifications of positions, and subsequently, we use saliency maps for those positions to suggest possible reasons for those failures.

While the bitboards provide lossless information, deriving valid piece trajectories directly require non-linear conditional logic that the linear probe cannot execute. The results show that the Transformer has not just preserved, but enhanced the features of the initial input representation. Surprisingly, even though the Knight's spatial reach ($d=2$) aligns with the $3 \times 3$ receptive fields, it performed poorly, suggesting that routing point-to-point interactions through convolutional kernels results in non-linear representations.

\textbf{Target Square Resolution.}\enskip The rule of not being able to capture friendly pieces is a fundamental rule in Chess. However, the representations formed by the CNN (and the MLP) are not linearly decodable, resulting in the high probabilities assigned to squares occupied by pieces of the same color as seen in \autoref{fig:linear_probe_comparison_bishop_cnn}. In contrast, the Transformer's latent space preserves this concept linearly, allowing the probe to suppress activations easily on friendly pieces as shown in \autoref{fig:linear_probe_comparison_bishop_transformer}.

\textbf{Piece occlusion.}\enskip The representations must contain linearly decodable information regarding piece occlusion (pieces blocking the line of sight of the target piece) for the probe to accurately determine long-range piece mobility. This conditional logic is not linearly accessible in the CNN's (and MLP's) latent representations, causing the probe to project "movement rays" directly through pieces, as seen in \autoref{fig:linear_probe_comparison_bishop_cnn}. Although the Transformer Probe depict arguably perfect results in \autoref{fig:linear_probe_comparison_bishop_transformer}, it often leaves residual probability on the immediate square behind the occluder before completely dropping to zero (see \hyperref[sec:appendix_linear_probes]{Appendix F}). 

\textbf{Spatial Fading.}\enskip In the CNN's latent space, the linearly accessible information regarding legal moves steadily decays as the Chebyshev distance from the target piece increases. We hypothesize that this is the result of feature  degradation caused by routing these long-range piece dynamics through multiple layers and overlapping receptive fields of the convolutional kernels. In contrast, the Transformer probe is able to output uniform and high probabilities for squares even after increasing distance, because it has established an $O(1)$ spatial routing path between any two squares within a single attention layer.

% --- Section 5: Discussion  ---
\section{Discussion}
Our findings present a paradoxical situation in which the Transformer, despite lacking spatial priors, forms a more linearly decodable spatial representation than the CNN that possesses spatial inductive biases. The ability to learn its own representation of the board state allows the Transformer to map the board into a latent space where spatial routing is unbounded by the physical limitations of the chessboard. While our empirical observations note that MLPs typically default to material counting for evaluation, and that CNNs can find simple long-range piece interactions (e.g. high gradients on a square with a hanging piece), they both struggle to highlight more complex long-range tactics. Our ablations (\autoref{cnn_classifier_head}) also show that replacing pooling with basic feature flattening yields no performance gain, confirming that the loss of spatial information is not entirely due to pooling but likely due to the entanglement of coordinates during the forward pass through overlapping receptive fields.

\textbf{Limitations.}\enskip Our formulation for Mean Spatial Reach cannot capture higher-order lines of sight (e.g., stacked Rooks). Positions that contain such interactions significantly affect the evaluation of positions, but they are suppressed by MSR and introduces variance into our correlation plots. Additionally, our analysis is bounded by a 1.2M parameter capacity constraint. While recent work has shown that Transformers can play at the Grandmaster level using only static evaluations at 270M parameters \cite{ruoss2024grandmaster}, it remains an open question on whether immense scaling of parameters for CNNs would eventually allow them to bypass the spatial routing bottlenecks we demonstrated in this paper and lead to a considerable gain in performance.

% --- Section 6: Conclusion  ---
\section{Conclusion}
Our work showed that while deep neural networks can possess the capacity to internalize spatial concepts without \textit{a priori} knowledge or search algorithms, the potential of these emergent representations depends heavily on their architectural inductive biases. Additionally, we show that localized convolutions result in entangled, non-linear representations, compared to the much more linearly decodable abstractions formed by the Transformer. Lastly, we show that the Transformer's ability to resolve scalable, long-range interactions that are independent of a position's combinatorial complexity, is the key driver for performance.

\subsubsection{Gaussian Label Smoothing}
\label{label_smoothing}
The centipawn score $E(s)$ is mapped to $c(E) \in [0, 6]$ with this interpolation function:
\[
c(E) =
\begin{cases}
\max\left(0.0, 0.5 - \frac{E - 500}{200}\right) & \text{if } E \ge 500 \\
1.5 - \frac{E - 300}{200} & \text{if } 300 \le E < 500 \\
2.5 - \frac{E - 100}{200} & \text{if } 100 \le E < 300 \\
3.5 - \frac{E + 100}{200} & \text{if } -100 \le E < 100 \\
4.5 - \frac{E + 300}{200} & \text{if } -300 \le E < -100 \\
5.5 - \frac{E + 500}{200} & \text{if } -500 < E \le -300 \\
\min\left(6.0, 5.5 - \frac{E + 500}{200}\right) & \text{if } E \le -500
\end{cases}\]
Then, we use a normalized Gaussian distribution centered at $c(E)$ to generate the soft target probability distribution of the 7 classes:
\[p_i = \frac{\exp\left( - \frac{(i - c(E))^2}{2\sigma^2} \right)}{\sum_{j=0}^{6} \exp\left( - \frac{(j - c(E))^2}{2\sigma^2} \right)}\]
The networks are trained to minimize the cross-entropy loss between their predictions and the soft target distribution. Here, we choose $\sigma = 0.6$ over standard label smoothing ($\sigma = 0.1$) to ensure the true target class retains a majority of the probability mass ($\approx66\%$) and also allocates a meaningful weight ($\approx16.5\%$ each) to the adjacent classes. This gave better results as shown below.

\subsubsection{CNN}
\label{cnn_details}
Let the main convolutional branch of a residual block be denoted as $\mathcal{F}$, where $\mathcal{F}(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{W}) = \text{BN}(\mathbf{W}_2 * \text{ReLU}(\text{BN}(\mathbf{W}_1 * \mathbf{x})))$. The forward pass for the $l$-th residual block is formally defined as:$$\mathbf{h}^{(l)} = \text{ReLU}\Big( \text{DropPath}\big(\text{SE}(\mathcal{F}(\mathbf{h}^{(l-1)}, \mathbf{W}^{(l)}))\big) + \mathbf{h}^{(l-1)} \Big)$$where $\mathbf{h}^{(l-1)}$ is the intermediate feature map from the previous layer.

During training, DropPath survival probabilities linearly decrease from $1.0$ at the first block to $0.8$ at the final block. The model we use for the main evaluation (SE-ResNet-16) consists of 16 residual blocks, a channel depth of $C=64$, and an SE reduction ratio of $r=8$. The output of the residual blocks is projected to a small MLP with hidden dimensions of 256 with a dropout of $0.5$ which then finally outputs the 7 class logits.

\subsubsection{Transformer}
\label{transformer_details}
Let MHA denote Multi-Head Self-Attention and FFN denote the Feed-Forward Network. The forward pass for the $l$-th encoder block using DeepNorm is formally defined as:
$$ \begin{aligned}
\mathbf{h}' &= \text{LN}\big(\mathbf{h}^{(l-1)} + \alpha \cdot \text{Dropout}(\text{MHA}(\mathbf{h}^{(l-1)}))\big) \\
\mathbf{h}^{(l)} &= \text{LN}\big(\mathbf{h}' + \alpha \cdot \text{Dropout}(\text{FFN}(\mathbf{h}'))\big)
\end{aligned} $$
where $\text{LN}$ is Layer Normalization and $\alpha$ is the DeepNorm residual scaling factor derived from the total network depth using the formula $\alpha = (2N)^{-0.25}$.

\subsection{Usage of MSR}
We measure the correlation between MSR and the performance advantage ($\Delta_{acc}$) between the Transformer (TF) and the CNN by first dividing the dataset into $B = 15$ equal-sized quantile bins. For each bin $b$, we calculate the average accuracy of both models:

\[Acc_{TF}^{(b)} = \frac{1}{|b|} \sum_{i \in b} \mathbbm{1}(\text{TF}_i = \text{True})\]

\[Acc_{CNN}^{(b)} = \frac{1}{|b|} \sum_{i \in b} \mathbbm{1}(\text{CNN}_i = \text{True})\]

Then, we take the percentage difference to get $\Delta_{acc}$ for each bin: 
\[\Delta_{Acc}^{(b)} = \left( Acc_{TF}^{(b)} - Acc_{CNN}^{(b)} \right) \times 100\]

We then calculate the correlation using the Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression formula:
\[r = \frac{\sum_{b=1}^{B} (X_b - \overline{X})(Y_b - \overline{Y})}{\sqrt{\sum_{b=1}^{B} (X_b - \overline{X})^2 \sum_{b=1}^{B} (Y_b - \overline{Y})^2}}\]
where $\overline{X}$ and $\overline{Y}$ are the means of the binned variables. 

\subsection{Data Stratification}
Our goal is to isolate spatial variance in positions. Here, we present the filtering pipeline on our open-board subset of positions to curate the ideal test environment where MSR shines. We start with the original uncontrolled subset of open-board positions. 
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, noitemsep]
    \item \textbf{Test 1:} The original uncontrolled subset of open-board positions. 
    \item \textbf{Test 2:} Restrict the Total Material Score on the board to $\in [0, 32]$.
    \item \textbf{Test 3:} Restrict the Total Piece Count to $\in [2, 16]$.
    \item \textbf{Test 4:} Restrict the Sliding Piece Ratio, which is the fraction of long-range pieces (Queen, Bishop, Rook) over total pieces to $\in [0.15, 0.50]$. This is our Spatial Variance Benchmark. With the last restriction, it ensures variance in MSR would be mainly due to the placement of the pieces rather than a trivial bias in piece types (e.g., a board containing more Queens than another).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Saliency Maps}
\label{sec:saliency_maps}
Unlike standard RGB images, where we commonly take the maximum gradient across the color channels, the inputs we described previously are sparse and mutually exclusive (a square can only contain one piece). To ensure we do not discard meaningful information about any potential piece when determining the total input sensitivity of each square, we construct the saliency map $M \in \mathbb{R}^{8 \times 8}$ by summing the magnitude of the gradients across all 12 piece channels:
\[M_{ij} = \sum_{k=1}^{12} | \mathbf{W}_{kij} |\]
where $\mathbf{W}_{kij}$ denotes the gradient element for piece channel $k$ at rank $i$ and file $j$.
