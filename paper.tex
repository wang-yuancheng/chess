\section{Introduction}
Deep learning has proved to be very capable in mastering complex tasks and solving challenging problems in a wide range of fields. However, deep neural networks are difficult to interpret and hence, are often viewed as black boxes. With the inclusion of domain specific knowledge in these networks, the distinction between architectural contributions and task specific optimizations becomes unclear. This makes it even more difficult for researchers to understand why these networks succeed, why some models perform better than others, and to what extent different foundational architectures contribute to solving domain specific problems. 

To explore how different models learn and interpret problems, we require an environment that is both challenging and well defined. A complex board game like chess serves as a perfect domain to seek a deeper understanding in common deep architectures. Chess offers an environment with perfect information, and along with a set of strict rules, it is possible to completely remove human intervention in model design. It also possess a vast state space of approximately $10^{46}$ unique states, forcing models to generalize and potentially discover patterns without being told. 

This leads to our central question: are deep networks able to internalize the geometry and dynamics of chess without context or search? If so, how differently do they reason with the limited information provided to them? 

In this paper,  we look into 3 main architectures: Multi Layer Perceptrons (MLPs), Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and Transformers. We investigate their differences in representation learning, specifically how different inherent inductive biases shape their learned representations of the board state under a strict \textit{tabula rasa} constraint. We show that models are able to learn geometric relationships via supervised learning when provided only the current state and a ground truth position evaluation from Stockfish.

\section{Related Work}
\subsection{Neural Chess Engines}
Initial breakthroughs in computer chess had shown that deep neural networks could replace manually tuned evaluation heuristics popular at that time\cite{lai2015giraffe}\cite{david2016deepchess}. Those early models utilized MLPs, which is remain as the backbone of Efficiently Updatable Neural Networks (NNUE) \cite{nasu2018efficiently} that powers state-of-the-art engines like Stockfish today. These engines leverage and improves on engineered input representations from previous generations such as piece to king relationships, along with advanced search algorithms to achieve superhuman play. Without these "add-ons", MLPs would see a pure 1D input vector and have to to learn chess concepts from scratch due to a lack of spatial information.

Later, CNNs emerged as a dominant architecture, as they are capable of capturing spatial relationships and 2D board geometry without the need for manual feature engineering \cite{silver2017alphazero}. AlphaZero, a chess engines arguably capable of defeating state-of-the-art engines back then, demonstrated that the usage of CNNs on chess evaluation functions can be very successful \cite{silver2017alphazero}. 

More recently, transformer architectures dominated the playing field, with open source implementations like Leela Chess Zero (Lc0) consistently winning 2nd place after Stockfish in online computer chess tournaments\cite{tcec_archive}. It was later shown that transformer based models can completely and efficiently internalize chess concepts, achieving grandmaster-level of play without any search algorithm or injection of domain specific knowledge \cite{ruoss2024grandmaster}.
